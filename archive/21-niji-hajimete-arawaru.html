虹始見

Niji hajimete arawaru

First rainbows appear

Seimei (清明)  ·  April 15

After the rain passes, the air holds a peculiar brightness — half-wet, half-lit, as if the sky cannot decide between weeping and smiling. You step outside and there it hangs: the season's first rainbow, its colors bleeding softly into the still-dripping eaves. The scent of soaked earth and cherry petals mingles with something electric, something clean.

Nature Notes

Swallows have arrived in earnest now, their forked tails scissoring through the humid air as they hunt insects stirred up by spring showers. The last mountain cherries shed their petals into swollen streams, while wisteria begins to unfurl its heavy purple racemes along temple walls. The sky alternates between dramatic cloud-towers and sudden clearings where light falls in cathedral shafts.

At the Table

Takenoko gohan

Rice cooked with young bamboo shoots and a whisper of soy — the dish captures the forest floor's brief, tender offering before the stalks harden.

Tai no shioyaki

Salt-grilled sea bream, its flesh at peak sweetness during spawning season, served whole as it has been for spring celebrations since ancient times.

Cultural Note

This period falls during the height of hanami's quieter second act, when people visit yama-zakura in the hills rather than fighting crowds beneath city somei-yoshino. It is also a traditional time for kaitori, the reading of the sky — farmers and fishermen watch the shape and position of rainbows to predict the coming days' weather, a practice now mostly forgotten but once essential to the planting calendar.

虹立ちて 子らの声する 水たまり

niji tachite / kora no koe suru / mizutamari

A rainbow rises — / children's voices carry from / the puddles below

By evening the colors will have dissolved into ordinary dusk, but the children will speak of the rainbow for days, as if they had witnessed something that chose to appear only for them.
